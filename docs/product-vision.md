# Product Vision — Bandwire

## 1. Vision & Mission

### Vision Statement

Every independent venue, grassroots promoter, and emerging band has access to a professional booking ecosystem that makes live music thrive — not just survive.

### Mission Statement

Bandwire replaces the broken patchwork of emails, DMs, spreadsheets, and guesswork with a single platform where venues find the right bands, send offers, sign contracts, and process payments — all in minutes.

### Founder's Why

Jon has spent 10 years on the other side of every broken booking email. As a gigging musician and guitarist of 15 years, he's lived the full spectrum of frustration: cold-emailing venues into the void, negotiating fees over Instagram DMs, tracking gigs in notes apps, and watching promising opportunities die because nobody could agree on terms fast enough.

But the insight that sparked Bandwire didn't come from the musician side alone. A decade of playing shows across dozens of venues gave Jon a rare perspective on how the other side operates — watching bookers juggle their inbox while pouring beers, seeing promoters manage their entire operation from a Google Sheet, and hearing venue owners complain about the same problems night after night: flaky bands, no-show acts, and calendars full of gaps.

Jon isn't a tech founder parachuting into the music industry with assumptions. He's from the grassroots scene Bandwire is built for. That means the product will be shaped by real experience, the community will trust it because it's built by one of their own, and the roadmap will be guided by someone who actually knows what matters in a green room at 11pm on a Friday.

### Core Values

**Ship for the 200-cap room, not the arena.** Every decision — pricing, features, design — should be evaluated against whether it serves an independent venue owner who books 3 shows a week with no assistant and no budget for enterprise software. If a feature only benefits large agencies, it can wait.

**Respect both sides of the stage.** Bandwire exists because the relationship between venues and musicians is broken. Every feature must make both sides feel respected, heard, and treated like professionals. No feature should benefit one side by degrading the experience of the other.

**Speed is a feature.** The #1 complaint in grassroots booking is that everything takes too long. If any workflow in Bandwire takes more than a few clicks or a few minutes, it's a bug. Friction is the enemy.

**Transparency over gatekeeping.** Musicians deserve to know why they weren't selected. Venues deserve to see real data about an artist's draw. Pricing should be clear, not hidden behind "contact sales." The whole industry suffers from opacity — Bandwire fixes that.

**Built in public, built with the scene.** Bandwire isn't a product dropped on the music industry from above. It's built alongside the people who use it — their feedback shapes the roadmap, their stories shape the brand, and their success is the only metric that matters.

### Strategic Pillars

**Full-loop over feature depth.** Bandwire's competitive advantage is handling the complete booking workflow — discovery through payment — in one place. It's better to have a simple version of every step than a sophisticated version of just one step. Depth comes later; the loop comes first.

**Venue-side value drives revenue; musician-side value drives growth.** Venues are the paying customers, so their workflow must be exceptionally smooth. Musicians are the supply that makes the platform valuable, so their onboarding must be effortless and their experience must feel like an opportunity, not a chore.

**One city at a time.** Network effects are local. A venue in Austin doesn't care how many bands are on the platform in Portland. Focus on density in one market before expanding. A city where Bandwire is essential is worth more than 50 cities where it's barely known.

**Earn trust before asking for money.** The freemium model exists because venue owners need to see Bandwire work before they'll pay for it. The free tier should be genuinely useful — not a crippled demo designed to frustrate people into upgrading.

### Success Looks Like

In 12 months, Bandwire is the default way independent venues and bands book gigs in 5+ cities. 200 venues actively use the platform, 1,500 musicians have profiles, and 500+ gigs have been booked through it. 30 venues pay for a subscription, generating approximately $2k/month in recurring revenue. When a new venue opens in one of Bandwire's markets and needs to start booking live music, someone in their network says "just get on Bandwire." Jon is fielding inbound requests from promoters in cities he hasn't launched in yet, and the phrase "Bandwire me" has started showing up in local music scene group chats.

## 2. User Research

### Primary Persona

**Jake, 35 — Independent Venue Owner**

Jake owns a 200-capacity live music bar in a mid-size city. He's the owner, the GM, and the booker — there's no separate talent buyer or booking agent. He hosts live music 3–4 nights a week because it drives bar revenue, but booking is the part of his job he dreads most.

His day starts around noon reviewing emails from bands he's never heard of, most of whom aren't a fit for his room. He spends 30 minutes scrolling through links, listening to snippets, and trying to figure out if this band from three towns over can actually draw 50 people on a Thursday. Most of the time he doesn't reply because he doesn't have time to write "not a fit" emails to 15 people. He feels guilty about the ghosting but there's no good alternative.

By mid-afternoon he's texting the same 8 bands he always books because finding new ones is exhausting. He tracks everything in a combination of Google Calendar (for dates), his email inbox (for confirmations), and a spreadsheet his old bar manager set up three years ago that nobody maintains properly. Double bookings happen at least once a quarter. Payment is Venmo or a check at the end of the night.

Jake is tech-comfortable — he uses Square for POS, Instagram for marketing, and Google Workspace for everything else. He'd switch to a new tool immediately if it saved him time, but he's been burned by software that promises simplicity and delivers complexity. He needs something that works in 5 minutes, not 5 hours of setup.

What would make him switch: seeing an actual booking happen faster than email. One successful gig booked through Bandwire in under 10 minutes and he's in.

### Secondary Personas

**Maria, 28 — Independent Promoter.** Maria promotes 2–3 shows a month at rented venues while holding down a day job. She finds bands through word of mouth and social media, manages everything in a Google Sheet, and loses sleep over whether her lineups will draw enough to cover costs. She's the ultimate power user for Bandwire — she needs discovery, booking workflow, and financial tracking all in one place. She'd pay for a tool that saves her 5 hours a week, but she needs it to be under $50/month.

**Marcus, 26 — Emerging Musician / Band Leader.** Marcus fronts a 4-piece indie rock band that plays regionally. They're past the "playing for free" stage but not yet big enough for an agent. Marcus spends hours every week emailing venues, building one-off EPKs in Google Docs, and following up on unanswered messages. He's demoralized by the ghosting and the feeling that getting gigs is about who you know, not how good you are. Bandwire represents a level playing field where his music and stats speak for themselves.

**Sam, 42 — Multi-Venue Operator.** Sam owns three bars that all host live music on weekends. He has different bookers at each location and no centralized view of what's happening across them. Acts get double-booked across his venues, communication falls through the cracks, and he can't tell which nights are profitable. He needs visibility and consistency — a single dashboard showing all three venues' booking status. He'd pay a premium for multi-venue management.

### Jobs To Be Done

**Functional jobs:**
When a venue has an open date, they need to find a band that fits their room's genre, capacity, and budget — and confirm the booking with agreed terms before someone else fills the date. When a band wants to play shows, they need to present themselves professionally to venues and get real booking opportunities without cold-emailing into the void. When a promoter is building a lineup, they need to compare available artists, evaluate draw potential, and lock in multiple bookings efficiently.

**Emotional jobs:**
Venue owners want to feel like they're running a professional operation, not duct-taping a booking system together from 6 different apps. Musicians want to feel respected and valued — like their talent matters more than their network. Promoters want to feel in control of their events instead of constantly anxious about whether everything will come together.

**Social jobs:**
Venue owners want to be known for great programming — a steady stream of good acts that draw crowds. Musicians want their peers to see them landing real gigs and building momentum. Promoters want to be seen as tastemakers who curate great lineups and treat artists fairly.

### Pain Points

**The email black hole (Critical, Daily).** Venues receive dozens of booking inquiries weekly and can't respond to most of them. Musicians send dozens of emails weekly and hear back from almost none. Both sides lose opportunities because the communication channel is fundamentally broken. The consequences are real: venues rebook the same stale rotation, musicians give up on outreach, and the local scene stagnates.

**Fragmented tools (Critical, Daily).** Booking management is scattered across email, DMs, spreadsheets, calendars, Venmo, and PDF contracts. Nothing is connected, so double bookings happen, payments get forgotten, and there's no single place to see the status of anything. Every venue owner has their own cobbled-together system and none of them work well.

**No quality signal (High, Weekly).** Venues have no reliable way to evaluate an unfamiliar band's draw potential. A Spotify page tells you about recorded music, not about how many people show up on a Wednesday night. Musicians have no way to demonstrate their live draw to venues who don't already know them. The result is risk-averse booking — venues stick with known acts.

**Overpriced or underpowered tools (High, Monthly).** Gigwell costs $199/month for unlimited capacity — that's a significant expense for a 200-cap bar. Prism has opaque pricing and is built for agencies, not solo bookers. On the other end, Gigmit and GigSalad help with discovery but have no booking workflow. There's nothing in between — affordable, simple, and full-featured.

**Unprofessional processes (Medium, Weekly).** Deals are made over text messages, terms are vague, contracts are inconsistent or nonexistent, and payment is often an afterthought. This lack of structure leads to disputes, no-shows, and a general feeling that the grassroots music industry doesn't take itself seriously. It's not that people don't want professionalism — they don't have the tools for it.

### Current Alternatives & Competitive Landscape

**Gigwell ($33–$199/month).** The closest direct competitor in terms of full-loop booking management. Strong on contracts, payments, and calendar management. Falls short on discovery — it's a management tool, not a marketplace. Pricing is prohibitive for most independent venues. Best for mid-size venues and agencies already doing high volume.

**Prism.fm (pricing not public).** All-in-one venue and promoter management. Comprehensive features but built for organizations, not individuals. Reports of poor customer responsiveness. More tool than platform — no marketplace component.

**Gigmit (free + Pro tier).** Two-sided marketplace popular in Europe. Good for festival applications. Weak on the actual booking workflow — once you find an act, you're back to email for everything else. Data-driven matching using streaming stats is a strong feature worth learning from.

**GigSalad (commission-based).** Large directory with 110,000+ acts. Works well for corporate events and private parties. Less relevant for the indie/grassroots live music scene. Commission model means both sides pay per transaction, which can create friction.

**The DIY stack (free).** Email + Google Calendar + spreadsheets + Venmo + Instagram DMs. This is what 80% of independent venues actually use. It's free, familiar, and terrible. Bandwire's real competitor isn't another software product — it's the inertia of "this is how we've always done it."

**Doing nothing.** Many venues rebook the same bands on rotation. Many musicians stop reaching out. The status quo is comfortable even though it's suboptimal. Bandwire must be meaningfully better than the current way, not just slightly better — otherwise the switching cost isn't worth it.

### Key Assumptions to Validate

**Assumption: Independent venue owners will pay for booking software.** We assume venues will convert from free to paid once they see value. To validate: offer a generous free tier, track usage, and measure conversion after 30/60/90 days. If venues use the product actively but won't pay, the value proposition or pricing needs adjustment.

**Assumption: Musicians will create quality profiles.** We assume bands will invest time building complete profiles with EPKs, media, and stats. To validate: track profile completion rates. If most profiles are half-finished, add incentives (visibility boost for complete profiles) or simplify the process.

**Assumption: Venues want to discover new bands, not just manage existing relationships.** We assume venues are frustrated by their limited rotation and want fresh acts. To validate: interview 10 venue owners about their booking habits. Some may prefer managing known acts over discovering unknown ones.

**Assumption: A single city can sustain enough supply and demand.** We assume that one metro area has enough venues and bands to create a viable marketplace. To validate: map the venue and band count in the launch city. If there are fewer than 50 active live music venues and 200 active bands, the market may be too thin.

**Assumption: Streaming stats are a useful proxy for live draw.** We assume Spotify/social data helps venues evaluate bands. To validate: compare streaming stats with actual attendance data from early bookings. The correlation may be weak, especially for local acts with strong in-person followings but modest online presence.

**Assumption: The freemium model generates enough revenue.** We assume 10–15% of active venues will convert to paid. To validate: track conversion rates and compare with SaaS benchmarks (typically 2–5% for freemium). If conversion is low, consider adjusting the free tier limits or pricing.

## 3. Product Strategy

### Product Principles

**The full loop wins.** Bandwire's advantage isn't any single feature — it's the fact that discovery, booking, contracts, and payment happen in one unbroken flow. Every product decision should strengthen the loop, not just one link in the chain. If adding a feature breaks the flow, it's not worth it.

**Two clicks, not ten.** Every workflow should be evaluated against "how many clicks does this take?" If a venue can't go from open date to sent offer in under 3 clicks, the design is too complex. If a musician can't respond to an offer in under 2 clicks, the UX has failed.

**Data earns trust.** Venues won't book unknown bands without evidence. Musicians won't use a platform that doesn't showcase their best work. Every profile, every match, every recommendation should be backed by real data — streaming numbers, past show ratings, genre fit scores. Gut feeling shouldn't be necessary.

**Free should be genuinely useful.** The free tier isn't a demo — it's a product. A venue should be able to book real gigs on the free tier. The upgrade should feel like unlocking more of something they already love, not like being released from a cage.

**Mobile-aware, desktop-first.** The primary workflow (posting dates, reviewing matches, sending offers) happens on desktop. But musicians check offers and respond on their phones. Every screen should work on mobile, even if it's optimized for desktop.

### Market Differentiation

Bandwire sits in a gap that no existing product fills. On one side, you have the full-featured booking management tools — Gigwell, Prism, OpenDate — built for agencies and mid-to-large venues. They're powerful, expensive, and overkill for an independent bar owner who books 3 acts a week. On the other side, you have the discovery platforms — Gigmit, GigSalad, Opnr — that help venues find bands but leave them back in email-land for the actual booking.

Bandwire is the only platform that combines discovery and full booking workflow at a price point and complexity level designed for the grassroots market. The differentiation is defensible for three reasons: the founder's authentic connection to the scene builds trust that no enterprise product can replicate, the full-loop approach creates switching costs once both sides of a booking are in the platform, and the city-by-city network effects mean that once Bandwire owns a local market, a competitor would need to rebuild that density from scratch.

### Magic Moment Design

The magic moment for venues is: "I just booked a Friday night act in under an hour — and I never opened my email." For musicians, it's: "I woke up to a real booking offer from a venue I didn't even know was looking."

For this to happen reliably, several things must be true in the product:

The matching algorithm must surface relevant bands within seconds of a venue posting an open date. This means genre, location, and availability data must be complete and accurate. Incomplete profiles can't show up in matches — which means the onboarding flow must strongly encourage profile completion.

The offer flow must be frictionless — one screen with pre-filled terms based on the venue's defaults, a single "Send Offer" button, and instant notification to the band. No email confirmation loops, no separate contract upload step.

The musician notification and response flow must work on mobile. If a band can't accept an offer from their phone in under 60 seconds, the magic moment is delayed and potentially lost.

The magic moment is achievable in the MVP if the core features are: open date posting, artist matching, offer workflow, and basic contract generation. Payment processing can be simplified (recording that payment will happen outside the platform) for v1 if full payment integration is too complex for the initial build.

### MVP Definition

**Open date posting and venue calendar.** Venues create profiles with their preferences (genres, capacity, typical budget, deal type) and post open dates with specific requirements. A simple calendar view shows upcoming dates and their booking status (open, hold, confirmed). This is the entry point for the entire product.

**Musician profiles with EPK.** Bands create profiles including: bio, genre tags, location, photos, music samples (embedded links), social/streaming links, and availability. Complete profiles are visible to venues in search and matching. Incomplete profiles are prompted to finish before they can appear in matches.

**Smart matching.** When a venue posts an open date, Bandwire surfaces matching bands based on genre, location (within a configurable radius), and availability. The matching is simple for v1 — tag-based filtering, not AI — but it should feel intelligent because it filters out irrelevant results.

**Offer and booking workflow.** Venues send offers to bands with terms (date, compensation, deal type, set time, load-in time). Bands can accept, decline, or counter with modified terms. Once both sides agree, the booking is confirmed and appears on both calendars.

**Basic contracts.** On booking confirmation, Bandwire auto-generates a simple performance agreement with the agreed terms. Both parties can view and acknowledge the contract. E-signature is a nice-to-have but not required for v1 — acknowledgment is sufficient.

**Notifications.** Email and in-app notifications for: new matches on an open date, new offers received, offer accepted/declined/countered, booking confirmed. Musicians especially need timely notifications since the speed of response is part of the magic moment.

### Explicitly Out of Scope

**Payment processing (v1).** Full in-app payment is deferred. The contract records the agreed payment terms, but actual money transfer happens outside the platform (Venmo, check, cash). This is how the industry works today and removing this friction isn't essential for proving the core value. Reconsider at 50+ active bookings/month.

**Advanced analytics.** Venue-side analytics (revenue per show, attendance tracking, genre performance) are valuable but not essential for the initial loop. Revisit in Phase 4 or when 30+ venues are actively booking.

**Booking agent dashboard.** Multi-artist management for agents and managers is a power-user feature that adds complexity. The MVP serves individual venue owners and individual bands. Revisit when agent users request it.

**Rating and review system.** Two-way ratings (venues rate bands, bands rate venues) are important for trust but add significant UX complexity. Defer until there's enough booking volume to generate meaningful reviews — approximately 100+ completed bookings.

**Streaming data integration.** Pulling live data from Spotify, Bandsintown, or social platforms is valuable for matching quality but adds API complexity and potential rate-limiting issues. The MVP uses self-reported data. Integrate streaming APIs when matching quality becomes the primary growth lever.

**Mobile native app.** The MVP is web-only (responsive). A native mobile app is planned for after product-market fit is validated.

### Feature Priority (MoSCoW)

**Must Have (P0):** Venue profiles and preferences, musician profiles with EPK, open date posting, tag-based matching, offer workflow (send/accept/decline/counter), booking confirmation with auto-generated contract, basic calendar view, email notifications, user authentication with role-based access (venue vs musician), responsive web design.

**Should Have (P1):** In-app notifications, search and browse (beyond just matching), musician availability calendar, venue deal templates (save and reuse common offer terms), profile completeness indicator, basic onboarding tutorial.

**Could Have (P2):** Streaming stats integration (Spotify, Bandsintown), two-way ratings and reviews, advanced matching (weighted scoring), booking analytics dashboard, multi-venue management, payment processing integration, export bookings to Google Calendar.

**Won't Have (this time):** Native mobile app, booking agent multi-artist dashboard, AI-powered recommendations, event promotion tools, ticketing integration, social features (messaging, community feed), white-label/API for third-party integration.

### Core User Flows

**Flow 1: Venue posts an open date and books a band.**
Trigger: Venue owner has an unfilled date on their calendar.
Steps: (1) Venue logs in, navigates to calendar, clicks on an open date. (2) Fills in date details: genre, budget, deal type, set time, any notes. (3) Posts the open date. (4) Bandwire surfaces matched bands based on criteria. (5) Venue reviews matched profiles — listens to samples, checks stats. (6) Venue sends an offer to chosen band with one click (terms pre-filled from date details). (7) Band receives notification, reviews offer, accepts. (8) Booking confirmed, contract auto-generated, both calendars updated.
Success criteria: Entire flow completes in under 10 minutes. At least 3 matched bands surface for a typical genre/location query.

**Flow 2: Musician creates a profile and receives first offer.**
Trigger: Musician hears about Bandwire and signs up.
Steps: (1) Musician signs up, selects "I'm a musician/band" role. (2) Completes profile: band name, bio, genre, location, photos, music links. (3) Sets availability on their calendar. (4) Profile goes live and appears in venue searches/matches. (5) Venue discovers the profile through matching or browsing. (6) Venue sends an offer. (7) Musician receives email + in-app notification. (8) Musician reviews offer, accepts or counters.
Success criteria: Profile creation under 5 minutes. First offer received within 7 days (dependent on marketplace density).

**Flow 3: Promoter browses and builds a lineup.**
Trigger: Promoter has a show date and needs to fill a lineup.
Steps: (1) Promoter logs in, navigates to browse/search. (2) Filters by genre, location, availability for the target date. (3) Reviews candidate profiles, shortlists favorites. (4) Sends offers to 2–3 bands for different slots (opener, headliner, etc.). (5) Bands respond. (6) Promoter confirms the final lineup. (7) Contracts generated for each confirmed act.
Success criteria: Full lineup booked in one session. Each offer takes under 2 minutes to send.

### Success Metrics

**Primary metric (North Star): Completed bookings per month.** This is the single number that matters most — it proves both sides of the marketplace are active and the product delivers its core value. Good: 10 bookings/month by month 2. Great: 50 bookings/month by month 3.

**Secondary metrics:**
Active venues (posted at least one open date in last 30 days). Good: 20 by month 2. Great: 50 by month 3.
Active musicians (profile complete and logged in within last 30 days). Good: 100 by month 2. Great: 300 by month 3.
Offer-to-booking conversion rate. Good: 40%. Great: 60%.
Paying subscribers. Good: 3 by month 3. Great: 5 by month 3.

**Leading indicators:**
Sign-ups per week (both sides). Profile completion rate. Time from sign-up to first action (posting a date or completing a profile). Match-to-offer rate (how often a surfaced match leads to an offer). Return visit rate (weekly active / monthly active).

### Risks

**Chicken-and-egg marketplace cold start (High likelihood, High impact).** Without bands, venues won't join. Without venues, bands won't join. Mitigation: Seed the musician side first by leveraging Jon's network. Personally onboard 10–15 venues in the launch city. Create fake demand if needed by posting open dates for partner venues.

**Venues won't pay (Medium likelihood, High impact).** Independent venues are cost-conscious and may use the free tier indefinitely. Mitigation: Set clear free tier limits (e.g., 3 bookings/month) that are useful for light users but insufficient for regular bookers. Demonstrate clear ROI in time savings.

**Low profile quality from musicians (Medium likelihood, Medium impact).** Musicians may create accounts but not complete profiles, resulting in poor matches. Mitigation: Require minimum profile completeness before appearing in search. Add a progress indicator showing profile strength. Consider featuring complete profiles more prominently.

**Existing tools improve (Low likelihood, Medium impact).** Gigwell or Prism could add a discovery marketplace. Gigmit could add booking workflow. Mitigation: Move fast, own the grassroots niche, and build community loyalty that large platforms can't replicate. Network effects in local markets create a moat.

**Scalability of manual city launches (Medium likelihood, Medium impact).** Each city requires hands-on seeding to build initial density. This doesn't scale. Mitigation: Document the launch playbook, find local ambassadors, and optimize for viral loops (venue recommends to musician, musician recommends to venue in another city).

**Technical execution risk (Medium likelihood, Medium impact).** Jon is not a developer and will rely heavily on AI coding tools. Complex features may take longer than expected. Mitigation: Keep the MVP ruthlessly simple. Use managed services (Convex, Clerk) that minimize backend complexity. Defer anything that isn't essential for the core booking loop.

**Regulatory/legal risk with contracts (Low likelihood, Low impact).** Auto-generated performance agreements may not be legally binding in all jurisdictions. Mitigation: Include a disclaimer that Bandwire contracts are templates, not legal advice. Consult a music industry attorney before launch for the standard terms template. Most grassroots gigs operate on informal agreements anyway — any structure is an improvement.

## 4. Brand Strategy

### Positioning Statement

For independent venue owners and grassroots promoters who are tired of managing bookings through email and spreadsheets, Bandwire is the live music booking platform that handles the full loop — discovery, offers, contracts, and payments — in one place. Unlike Gigwell and Prism, Bandwire is built for the grassroots scene by someone who's lived it, at a price that makes sense for a 200-cap bar.

### Brand Personality

Bandwire is the sharp indie operator. Think of a person who runs a respected independent record label — they know the industry inside out, they're efficient and no-nonsense, but they're also genuinely passionate about the music. They wear clean, simple clothes (no suits, no band tees with holes). They respond to emails quickly and clearly. They don't namedrop or oversell. They let their work speak for itself.

In conversation, Bandwire is the person who cuts through industry BS with a direct answer. They're friendly without being eager. They'll tell you if something isn't going to work, but they'll also help you find what will. They respect your time because they value their own.

What Bandwire would never do: use jargon to sound impressive, make promises it can't keep, add unnecessary features to look "enterprise," or pretend the grassroots scene is smaller than it is.

### Voice & Tone Guide

The voice is constant: **friendly, efficient, confident.** Warm enough to feel human, concise enough to respect people's time. The tone shifts slightly based on context — a bit more encouraging during onboarding, a bit more direct in error states, a bit more energetic in success moments.

| Context | DO | DON'T |
|---|---|---|
| **Onboarding** | "Let's get your venue set up. This takes about 3 minutes." | "Welcome to the Bandwire experience! We're SO excited to have you on board!!!" |
| **Error state** | "This one didn't work out — the band passed. Here are 3 more matches." | "Oops! Something went wrong. Please try again later." |
| **Empty state** | "No matches yet. Try widening your genre or budget range." | "It looks like there's nothing here! Why not explore our amazing features?" |
| **Success** | "You're locked in! Contract's on its way to both sides." | "Congratulations! You have successfully completed a booking transaction!" |
| **Marketing copy** | "Book live music in minutes, not weeks." | "Leverage our proprietary matching algorithm to optimize your booking pipeline." |
| **Upgrade prompt** | "You've hit your monthly limit. Upgrade to keep booking." | "UNLOCK PREMIUM FEATURES NOW! Don't miss out on this exclusive offer!" |
| **Notification** | "New offer from The Blue Room — $400 flat, next Friday." | "You have a new notification! Click here to view details." |

I built this because I've been a gigging musician for 10 years. I've been on the other side of every broken booking email. The tools that exist are either too expensive for indie venues or they stop at discovery and leave you back in email for the actual booking.

We're launching city by city, starting with our home market. We're free for musicians, freemium for venues, and we're already seeing venues book their first shows through the platform in under an hour. We're looking for venue partners in [city] who want to be early adopters."

### Competitive Differentiation Narrative

The live music booking market is split into two worlds that don't talk to each other. On one side, you have enterprise booking software — Gigwell at $199/month, Prism with opaque pricing, OpenDate bundled with ticketing. These tools are built for mid-size venues with dedicated talent buyers and agencies managing hundreds of shows a year. They're powerful, but they're overkill for the independent venue owner who books 3 acts a week between pouring drinks and fixing the PA system.

On the other side, you have discovery platforms — Gigmit, GigSalad, Opnr. These help venues find bands, but the moment a match is made, you're back to email. There's no booking workflow, no contracts, no payment handling. They solve half the problem and leave you to figure out the harder half yourself.

Bandwire is the first platform built specifically for the grassroots market that handles the complete booking loop: discovery, matching, offers, negotiation, contracts, and payment — all in one place. It's designed for the Jake who owns a 200-cap bar and the Marcus who fronts a 4-piece band playing regionally. And it's built by someone who's actually been in the green room — not a tech founder reading about the music industry from a pitch deck.

### Brand Anti-Patterns

**Never sound corporate.** Banned words: leverage, synergy, solution, optimize, pipeline, stakeholder, scalable (in marketing copy), end-to-end (in marketing copy). Never use stock photography — especially not people in suits, people pointing at screens, or generic "diverse team" shots. If an image doesn't look like it could be taken at an actual gig, it doesn't belong on Bandwire.

**Never be cluttered.** No dashboard should show more than 5 key metrics at once. No onboarding flow should have more than 4 steps. No page should have more than 2 calls to action. If the interface feels like it needs a training manual, it needs to be simplified.

**Never gatekeep or manipulate.** No dark patterns in the upgrade flow — no "are you sure you want to miss out?" popups. No hiding features behind upgrade walls without clear explanation. No artificial scarcity ("only 3 spots left!"). No aggressive email campaigns. If someone wants to use the free tier forever, that's fine — they're still contributing to the marketplace.

**Never patronize musicians.** Musicians are professionals, not supplicants. The platform should never make them feel like they're begging for gigs. No "submit your application and wait" language — it should feel like a conversation, not a casting call. Rejection should be transparent and respectful.

## 5. Design Direction

### Design Philosophy

**Dark canvas, bright signals.** The dark background is the stage — it recedes and lets the content shine. Color is used sparingly and deliberately, like stage lights cutting through a dark room. Bright accents draw attention to the things that matter: open dates, new offers, confirmed bookings.

**Density where it counts, space where it breathes.** Venue owners are making decisions — they need to see data at a glance (calendar, matches, stats). But the interface should never feel cramped. Use density for functional areas (calendars, lists, profiles) and generous spacing for emotional areas (landing page, onboarding, confirmation screens).

**Motion suggests life, not decoration.** Animations should feel like something is happening — a new match appearing, a booking being confirmed, a notification arriving. They should never feel like decoration or delay. If an animation doesn't communicate information, it doesn't belong.

**Every component earns its place.** No decorative elements that don't serve a function. No gradients for the sake of gradients. No borders that don't separate meaningful content areas. If removing an element doesn't make the page worse, remove it.

### Visual Mood

The overall aesthetic is a modern, dark-themed interface with electric accents — think of Linear's precision and information density meets the energy of a gig poster. The dark background creates a sense of focus and atmosphere, like being backstage before a show. Vibrant accent colors — electric blue and neon purple — create moments of energy and draw attention to key interactive elements.

This isn't a moody, grungy aesthetic. It's clean, modern, and sharp. The closest references would be Linear (for information architecture and density), Vercel's dashboard (for dark theme execution), and Raycast (for the balance of power and simplicity). The music-scene energy comes through in the accent colors and occasional typographic boldness, not through visual cliches like guitar illustrations or music note icons.

### Component Philosophy

**Border radius:** Consistent `--radius-sm: 6px` for small elements (badges, tags), `--radius-md: 8px` for standard components (cards, inputs, buttons), `--radius-lg: 12px` for larger containers (modals, popovers), `--radius-full: 9999px` for circular elements (avatars, status dots).

**Shadows:** Minimal shadow usage on dark backgrounds — they don't read well. Instead, use subtle borders (`--color-border`) and surface color differentiation to create depth. When shadows are needed (modals, dropdowns), use a faint glow effect: `0 4px 24px rgba(59, 130, 246, 0.08)`.

**Buttons:** Primary buttons use `--color-primary` with white text. Secondary buttons use transparent backgrounds with `--color-border` borders and `--color-text` text. Ghost buttons have no border or background — just text color. All buttons have `--radius-md` border radius, `--space-2` vertical padding, `--space-4` horizontal padding. Minimum touch target: 44x44px.

**Inputs:** Dark surface background (`--color-surface`), subtle border (`--color-border`), white text. Focus state adds `--color-primary` border with a faint glow ring. Placeholder text uses `--color-text-muted`. Minimum height: 40px. Padding: `--space-3` horizontal.

**Cards:** Surface background, `--color-border` border, `--radius-md` corners, `--space-5` padding. Hover state lightens to `--color-surface-hover`. Cards should never have more than 3 levels of visual hierarchy inside them.

### Iconography & Imagery

**Icon library: Lucide React.** Clean, consistent, outline-style icons. 24px default size with 1.5px stroke width. Use icons to support text labels, not replace them — every icon should be accompanied by text in primary navigation.

**Icon colors:** Default to `--color-text-muted`. Active/interactive icons use `--color-text`. On hover, icons transition to `--color-primary`.

**Photography/imagery:** No stock photos — ever. Product imagery should be screenshots or illustrations of the actual interface. If lifestyle imagery is needed for marketing, use authentic photos from real gigs and venues (with permission). The aesthetic should be real, not polished — a phone photo from the sound booth beats a professional stock shot of a concert.

**Illustrations:** Minimal. If used, simple line-art style consistent with the icon library. No cartoon-style illustrations or mascots. The product is the visual — let the UI speak for itself.

### Accessibility Commitments

**WCAG 2.1 AA compliance** is the target. Specific commitments:

**Color contrast:** All text meets 4.5:1 contrast ratio against its background (AA). Large text (18px+) meets 3:1. The dark theme is tested against these ratios — the chosen palette meets these thresholds.

**Keyboard navigation:** All interactive elements are reachable via Tab. Focus indicators are visible — a 2px ring using `--color-primary` with 2px offset. Focus order follows visual layout. No keyboard traps.

**Screen reader support:** All images have alt text. Form inputs have associated labels. Dynamic content changes are announced via ARIA live regions. Navigation landmarks are properly marked up.

**Minimum touch targets:** 44x44px for all interactive elements on touch devices.

**Reduced motion:** Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` media query. Users with this preference get instant state changes instead of animations.

### Motion & Interaction

**Default transition:** `150ms ease-out` for hover/focus states. Quick enough to feel responsive, smooth enough to not feel instant.

**Page transitions:** None. Pages load immediately — no page-level animations. Content within pages can fade in with `200ms ease-out` on initial load.

**Micro-interactions:** Buttons have a subtle scale effect on press (`transform: scale(0.98)`). Cards have a slight background color shift on hover. New items (matches, notifications) can slide in from the right with `250ms ease-out`.

**Loading states:** Use skeleton screens (surface-colored placeholder shapes) for content loading, not spinners. Spinners are reserved for short, indeterminate actions (sending an offer, generating a contract). The skeleton should match the layout of the expected content.

**What does NOT animate:** Navigation, form submissions, data table updates, modals opening/closing (they appear instantly). Only transitions that communicate "something new happened" should animate.
